Crew Site Log: The Complete Setup Guide

Everything you need to set up automatic arrival and departure logging for your crew, get the records to the office, and fix it when something looks wrong. Bookmark this page; it is written to come back to.

How it works

Every saved job site in FieldWatt gets an invisible circle drawn around it, called a geofence. The iPhone itself watches those circles using the same low power system Apple uses for reminders like "notify me when I get home." When the phone crosses into a circle, FieldWatt records an arrival; when it crosses out, a departure. This works with the app closed, the phone locked, and the phone in a pocket, day or night.

The phone logs one event crossing in and one crossing out. Nothing is recorded along the rest of the route.

What it records, and what it never records

It records
  • The time the phone entered a saved site's circle
  • The time the phone left that circle
  • The site name and the technician name from the profile
It never records
  • Routes, driving speed, or continuous position
  • Anywhere that is not a saved job site
  • Anything while the feature is switched off

Events live on the technician's own phone, visible to them in the app at any time. They leave the phone exactly two ways: log emails the technician sends, and, if the company sets one up, automatic posts to the company's own automation address (covered below). FieldWatt has no server and the developer never receives any location data.

1Get the coordinates for each site

Each site needs latitude and longitude in decimal form, for example 30.2672, −97.7431. Two easy ways to get them:

Apple Maps (iPhone):

  1. Search the site address, or find the spot on the satellite view.
  2. Touch and hold the exact spot where the crew actually works (the laydown yard or array, not the mailbox at the road). A pin drops.
  3. Swipe up on the place card. The coordinates appear near the bottom. Tap them to copy.

Google Maps (phone or computer):

  1. Press and hold the spot (right click on a computer). The coordinates show in the pin's info card or at the top of the menu.
  2. Tap or click them to copy.
Common mistakes: pasting the address instead of numbers; swapping the two numbers (latitude comes first and is between −90 and 90; in the US it is the positive one); using degrees minutes seconds like 30°16'02"N instead of decimals. If your maps app shows the degree format, look for a decimal option or use Google Maps, which copies decimals.
Drop the pin where the trucks park. On a big site, put the center on the work area or gate the crew actually passes through, not the geometric center of a 500 acre parcel.

2Choose the radius

The radius is how far from the pin the circle reaches, in meters. Bigger circles are more reliable; smaller circles are more precise. Phones locate themselves within roughly 20 to 50 meters in the open, worse in vehicles and bad weather, so a circle that hugs a building too tightly causes missed or repeated events.

Radius guidance at a glance.
Site typeSuggested radius
Residential rooftop job100 to 150 m (do not go below 100)
Commercial building or plaza200 to 300 m
Storage yard or shop150 to 250 m
Utility scale solar site300 to 500 m, centered on the gate or laydown

FieldWatt defaults to 200 m, a good starting point for most work. Departures use a built in buffer, so a tech standing near the edge does not flap in and out.

3Enter it in FieldWatt

  1. Open Site Forms → Company, crew, sites, recipients (also reachable from Settings).
  2. Tap the site (or Add site).
  3. Paste the coordinates into GPS coordinates, in the form latitude, longitude, decimals with a comma between.
  4. Set the Geofence radius just below it.
  5. While you are there, fill your name and email and the company email under Company. The log emails use them.
The Crew Site Log screen shows "Sites monitored: X of Y." If a site is missing from X, its GPS field did not parse; recheck the format.

4Turn it on (each phone)

  1. Open Settings → Crew Site Log (or Site Forms → Crew Site Log).
  2. Read the consent screen and tap I understand and agree.
  3. Flip on Log my site entries and exits.
  4. iOS asks for location. Choose Allow While Using the App if that is the only option shown, then finish with the step below, because background logging needs Always.
  5. Go to iPhone Settings → Apps → FieldWatt → Location and select Always, and make sure Precise Location is on. iOS often grants Always in two stages and may pop a confirmation a few days later asking to keep allowing it; tap Always Allow.
  6. Back in FieldWatt, the screen should read Location permission: Always (correct).
If permission shows "While using only," events will only log while the app is open on screen, which defeats the point. The Always grant is the single most common setup miss.

5Get consent signed

Open Site Forms → Location Tracking Consent. It states in plain language what is and is not recorded, includes the monitoring hours (pick All hours if your crew works nights), and captures the employee and company signatures. Generate the PDF and keep it with your HR records: one per employee, before their phone starts logging. Several states require this in writing; all of them make it a better conversation to have up front.

Getting the record, option A: the daily log email

Zero setup. At the end of the day the technician opens Crew Site Log and taps Email today's log. A prefilled email with every arrival and departure goes to the company email and the technician's own email from the profile. With no signal, it waits in the Outbox and sends when coverage returns. Good enough for timesheet backup and daily accountability.

Getting the record, option B: instant alerts

For an email the moment someone arrives or leaves, the phone posts each event to an automation URL your company owns, and that automation sends the email. FieldWatt has no server, so this uses yours, and the free tier of any of these services is plenty.

Both delivery paths. Use either or both. If the phone has no signal at the site, posts queue and fire when coverage returns.

Each post is JSON like this (events batch up if several are pending):

{ "events": [ { "event": "enter", "site": "Lakeside Plaza Roof", "technician": "Marcus Reed", "time": "2026-07-07T14:32:05Z" } ] }

Easiest: Google Apps Script (free, ten minutes).

  1. Go to script.google.com while signed into the company Google account and create a new project.
  2. Replace the contents with the script below, and put the boss's email in.
  3. Click Deploy → New deployment → Web app. Set Execute as Me and Who has access to Anyone. Deploy and approve the permissions.
  4. Copy the web app URL it gives you and paste it into FieldWatt's Company automation URL field on the Crew Site Log screen, on each phone.
function doPost(e) { var data = JSON.parse(e.postData.contents); var events = data.events || []; for (var i = 0; i < events.length; i++) { var ev = events[i]; var verb = ev.event === "enter" ? "arrived at" : "left"; var when = new Date(ev.time).toLocaleString("en-US", { timeZone: "America/Chicago" }); MailApp.sendEmail({ to: "boss@yourcompany.com", subject: ev.technician + " " + verb + " " + ev.site, body: ev.technician + " " + verb + " " + ev.site + " at " + when }); } return ContentService.createTextOutput("ok"); }

Change the time zone string if you are not Central. To add recipients, make the "to" field a comma separated list.

Alternative: Zapier or Make. Create a new Zap with the trigger Webhooks by Zapier → Catch Hook, copy the hook URL into FieldWatt, send one test event (see First test below), then map the fields into a Send Email action. Note the payload wraps events in a list, so in Zapier map from events 1 event, events 1 site, and so on. Make works the same way with its Webhooks module.

The URL must start with https. Every technician's phone needs the same URL pasted once; after that it is automatic.

Testing it the first time

  1. Set up one site you can drive to, with a 200 m radius.
  2. Enable logging on one phone, confirm Always (correct) and Sites monitored: 1 of 1.
  3. Start from at least a half mile away, drive to the site, park inside the circle for a few minutes, then leave.
  4. Expect the arrival notification within a few moments of crossing, sometimes up to two or three minutes; iOS trades a little speed for battery. The departure fires a bit beyond the circle's edge by design.
  5. Check the event list in the app, tap Email today's log, and if you set an automation URL, look for the bolt icon on each event showing it posted.
If the phone was already inside the circle when you enabled logging, no arrival fires until it leaves and comes back. Always test with a real crossing.

Troubleshooting

SymptomCheck, in order
No events ever logPermission must say Always with Precise Location on. The site must appear in "Sites monitored." Coordinates must be decimals with the comma. The toggle must be on, and the phone must actually cross the boundary, not start inside it.
Events log lateNormal within 2 to 3 minutes. Low Power Mode slows detection further. Rural areas with no cell towers rely on GPS alone and can be slower.
Repeated arrive and depart at one visitRadius too small for the site or for local GPS quality. Double it and retest. Parking exactly on the boundary line will always be noisy.
Wrong site loggedTwo circles overlap. Shrink radii or move pins apart until they do not touch.
Automation emails not arrivingURL must be https and pasted exactly. Look for the bolt icon on events: no bolt means the post has not succeeded yet (it retries). Test the hook by visiting your Zap history or the Apps Script executions page. Check spam.
Events stopped after a few daysiOS asked "keep allowing location?" and someone tapped the wrong choice. Recheck iPhone Settings → Apps → FieldWatt → Location → Always. Also confirm the employee did not toggle the feature off.
"Sites monitored: 0"No site has valid coordinates, or the toggle is off, or permission was denied. The screen tells you which with a warning line.
More than 20 sitesiOS watches at most 20 per phone. FieldWatt monitors the first 20 with coordinates and warns about the rest. Clear coordinates from finished jobs to make room.

FAQ

Does it kill the battery? No. Geofencing uses the same low power monitoring as Apple's own location reminders. It is not GPS navigation running all day; typical cost is a few percent.

Does it work if the phone is off or dead? No. No phone, no log. Airplane mode usually still detects crossings (GPS still works) but deliveries queue until signal returns.

Can an employee turn it off? Yes, always: the toggle in the app, or revoking location permission in iOS Settings. That is by design; the answer to a tech quietly disabling it is a management conversation backed by the signed consent form, not software.

Does the boss see where techs are between sites? No. Only crossings of saved site circles exist anywhere. There is no map, no route, no live view.

Night work? Fine. Monitoring runs around the clock; note it in the consent form's monitoring hours field.

Several crews and many phones? Each phone logs itself independently and posts to the same automation URL, so the office email stream covers everyone. Each phone's technician name comes from its own profile, so set that on every phone.

Do sites sync between phones? No; FieldWatt has no server by design. Enter the sites on each phone once (a few minutes with the coordinates in a text message), or set them up on one phone at the office before handing it out.

What about iPads or Android? iPhones only for the site log. iPads mostly lack the always on location hardware setup in the field, and FieldWatt does not run on Android.

Quick reference: coordinates as decimals with a comma · radius 200 m default, bigger for big sites · permission must say Always with Precise on · consent form signed first · daily email needs nothing · instant alerts need the automation URL · test with a real drive in and out.
